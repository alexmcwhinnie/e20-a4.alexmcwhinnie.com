The Australorp

The Black Australorp (originally named the Black Orpington) was bred in Australia from the same stock that created the British Black Orpington. Due to the practicality of the Australian breeders, the Black Australorp’s focus was more on egg production than for their meat. This in turn created a schism between these two birds of the commonwealth, and in the 1920s their name was created to reflect their new breed.

Black Australorps are renowned as world record egg layers and exceptional mother hens. They are not quite as known for their meat, which may be part of the reason that their popularity went on the decline.

These birds are incredibly docile and can be rather affectionate. Despite their origins in the tropical Australia, the Black Australorp is very adaptive and can thrive in harsh winters, although their egg production does suffer due to the shortened days. These birds are currently in recovery.

The Wyandotte

The Wyandotte first came into prominence in the late 1800s. Named after the Native American tribe situated in the northern reaches of New York and parts of Ontario, Canada.

The Wyandotte is recognized as a good dual-purpose bird, providing ample eggs and good for meat. They are very calm in their temperament and tend to be very attentive and good mothers. They are very cold hardy, and their flat rose comb is very well suited for harsh winters.

They are known for their ‘laced’ feather patters, and rather prized for the silvery-blue color patterns, but their color variations can be quite vast. There are at least 15 known color variants of the Wyandotte, though some are not recognized as true Wyandottes by the American Poultry Association.

Wyandotte numbers were on the decline, but this breed is currently in recovery.

The Rhode Island Red

The Rhode Island Red is the definition of a hard weathered and hard working bird. This breed was developed in the late 1800s in Massachusetts. The primary focus was a ‘dual purpose’ bird. The hen’s high egg production, coupled with it’s size, meant that this bird would provide families with a steady stream of eggs, and a hearty dressed meal.

Once considered the pinnacle of flavor, this bird saw its numbers decline due to the advent of modern farming and battery conditions. The Rhode Island Red is typically used as part of selective breeding due to the high capacity egg production.

Through the resurgence of backyard farming, the Rhode Island Red’s status is in recovery. The gentle bird that can withstand hard winters, is proving to still be a household favorite.

Rhode Island Red Facts
